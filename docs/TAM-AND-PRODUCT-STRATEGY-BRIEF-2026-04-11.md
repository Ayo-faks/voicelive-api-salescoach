# Wulo TAM And Product Strategy Brief

## Current Situation

Wulo has been built primarily from secondary research, online signals, competitor review, and AI-assisted synthesis rather than direct customer usage data. The product appears directionally aligned with a real market need, but it is still in a hypothesis stage until therapists use it in practice. The immediate goal is not to produce a perfect TAM model. It is to build a credible assumption-led market model, then tighten it with pilot evidence.

## Core Questions

1. How do we estimate TAM, SAM, and SOM before we have customers?
2. How do we compare growth paths such as inbound versus outbound acquisition?
3. What pricing structure should we test first: per therapist seat, per child, per school, or cohort-based?
4. Should product strategy be top-down or bottom-up?
5. Where does Wulo currently stand, given the product was built before direct user input?

## Recommended Framing

### 1. TAM should be modeled in two layers

Use both:

- A top-down TAM for investor storytelling.
- A bottom-up SAM and SOM for real operating decisions.

For Wulo, top-down TAM can start with the number of children with speech and language needs, the number of therapists, the number of schools with SEND provision, and relevant NHS or local authority budgets. That gives a broad market narrative.

The bottom-up model matters more right now. Estimate:

- Number of therapists you can realistically reach in 12 to 24 months.
- Number of schools or clinics likely to run pilots.
- Expected conversion from outreach to pilot.
- Expected conversion from pilot to paid usage.
- Expected annual revenue per therapist, school, or cohort.

This bottom-up view is the practical answer to market size when there are no customers yet.

### 2. The delta between inbound and outbound should be treated as a scenario model

Without customer history, the inbound versus outbound delta cannot be measured directly. It has to be estimated through assumptions and then updated from pilot data.

A simple first model:

- Inbound: content, founder-led thought leadership, clinical credibility, referrals, therapist communities.
- Outbound: direct outreach to therapists, schools, SENCOs, clinics, and local authorities.

For each channel, track assumed and then actual values for:

- Cost to reach one qualified lead.
- Conversion to discovery call.
- Conversion to pilot.
- Conversion to paid account.
- Sales cycle length.
- Average contract value.

The "delta" is not just lead volume. It is the difference in CAC, conversion quality, speed to close, and retention potential. Early on, outbound usually creates learning faster, while inbound usually scales more efficiently later.

### 3. Pricing should start from the buyer, not the product feature list

Wulo likely has more than one buyer type, so one pricing model may not fit all segments.

Recommended first-pass pricing tests:

- Therapist private practice: per seat or per therapist subscription.
- Schools: annual site license or cohort-based pricing.
- Local authorities or NHS pilots: pilot contract with defined cohort size and review metrics.

For early testing, per-seat is the simplest starting point because it is easy to explain, easy to bill, and easy to compare against therapist income. But per-seat alone may underprice value for schools or institutional buyers. The likely long-term structure is mixed:

- Per therapist seat for independents.
- Per school or per cohort for institutions.

### 4. Product strategy should now shift from research-led to pilot-led

The product so far has been built top-down from market research and problem analysis. That was reasonable for reaching a first usable prototype, especially in a market where the founder needed to move quickly before customer access was in place.

However, the next stage should be bottom-up:

- Observe how therapists actually use the workflow.
- Learn what parts of the product create trust or resistance.
- Find where usage breaks down in a real session.
- Identify which feature is truly core versus merely interesting.

The right sequence now is:

1. Keep the high-level thesis.
2. Pilot with therapists.
3. Collect workflow, pricing, and outcome evidence.
4. Narrow the product around the strongest repeated value signal.

### 5. Current assessment of Wulo's product-building strategy

Wulo is not starting from zero. It is beyond idea stage and already has a substantial product surface. But it is still pre-product-market-fit because the feature set has been shaped more by inferred need than repeated user behavior.

That means the current position is best described as:

- Strong research-led prototype.
- Plausible market thesis.
- Unvalidated buying behavior.
- Entering design-partner and pilot validation stage.

This is not a failure of strategy. It is a normal transition point. The risk is only if the team continues adding features before learning which user, workflow, and pricing motion actually converts.

## Working Conclusions

- Use top-down TAM for narrative, but manage the business from bottom-up SAM and SOM.
- Treat inbound versus outbound as a channel experiment, not a theoretical debate.
- Start pricing tests with per-seat for therapists and pilot/site pricing for schools.
- Shift product development from research-led building to therapist-led iteration.
- Judge the next phase by pilot conversion, retention, workflow adoption, and willingness to pay.

## Immediate Next Steps

1. Build a simple market model with three scenarios: conservative, base, aggressive.
2. Define one primary pilot segment first: independent therapists or schools, not both equally.
3. Run 5 to 10 therapist pilots focused on workflow learning, not feature breadth.
4. Capture pricing feedback during pilots, even before formal charging starts.
5. After the first pilots, update TAM, pricing, and channel assumptions using real conversion data.

## One-Sentence Summary

Wulo should stop treating TAM, pricing, and product strategy as abstract planning questions and instead use therapist pilots to convert research-based assumptions into evidence-based decisions.
