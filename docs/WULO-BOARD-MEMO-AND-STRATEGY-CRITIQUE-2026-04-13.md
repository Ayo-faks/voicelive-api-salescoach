# Wulo Board Memo And Strategy Critique

Prepared: 2026-04-13

This document contains two items only:

1. A blunt board-style memo on market, wedge, moat, and risks.
2. A Google-style product strategy critique covering what to cut, focus, and scale first.

## Board Memo

### Executive View

Wulo is not a broad AI education company right now. It is a vertical workflow SaaS business for paediatric speech and language therapy.

The product experience is child-facing, but the economic value is therapist-facing. The business only works if therapists see Wulo as a practical way to extend targeted practice between sessions without increasing review burden.

The market pain is real. The product thesis is credible. The company risk is that it may build too much before proving one repeatable buying motion.

### Market

The market is large enough and painful enough to matter, but need is not the same as adoption.

The strongest market thesis is not "AI for children with SEN." It is "a between-session workflow tool for speech and language therapists."

That is smaller, but much more investable.

The correct buyer sequence is:

1. Independent paediatric SLTs and small clinics.
2. SEND schools.
3. Local authorities and NHS providers.

That order matters because therapists decide quickly, give direct workflow feedback, and create the earliest proof of value. Schools and public-sector buyers come later, after trust and outcomes exist.

### Wedge

The wedge is not the avatar.

The wedge is not AI by itself.

The wedge is therapist-directed between-session practice plus structured review.

Wulo wins only if a therapist can say:

- My children practise more.
- I can see what happened without replaying everything.
- I can prepare the next session faster and with more confidence.

If those statements are not true, the rest of the product does not matter.

### Moat

There is no durable moat yet.

There is a plausible future moat, but it has not been earned.

If Wulo builds one, it will come from:

- therapist trust
- clinically credible product behaviour
- safeguarding and consent posture
- longitudinal supervised practice data
- a review and planning loop that improves as more sessions happen
- deep workflow fit inside therapist, clinic, and school operations

The model layer is not the moat. Voice AI, scoring, and planning capabilities will continue to commoditise.

If Wulo becomes defensible, it will be because it owns trust, workflow, and data in a narrow vertical.

### Risks

#### 1. Product Sprawl Before Proof

The product surface is already broad: live sessions, memory, recommendations, plans, dashboards, invites, workspaces, consent flows, data export, and more.

That is impressive, but it is also dangerous. A company can still lose by building a complete system for a customer that has not yet committed to buying the core loop.

#### 2. Buyer Confusion

Wulo can be described as a child app, a therapist tool, a school support product, a waiting-list solution, an AI practice buddy, or an analytics platform.

That is too many stories. Early-stage companies usually lose when they allow the market to choose among multiple narratives instead of enforcing one.

#### 3. Novelty Risk

Children may enjoy the buddy and therapists may like the demo. That is not enough.

The real question is whether weekly use continues after novelty fades.

#### 4. Clinical Trust Gap

The positioning correctly avoids diagnostic claims, but healthcare-adjacent buyers still need evidence, governance, and predictable product behaviour.

Without clinical credibility, Wulo risks being seen as clever rather than essential.

#### 5. Institutional Sales Drag

Schools, local authorities, and NHS buyers are attractive because the budgets are larger.

They are also slower, more political, and more compliance-heavy. If Wulo leans into them too early, it can spend a year looking busy while learning very little.

#### 6. Weak Proof Of Willingness To Pay

The product is live, but direct customer validation is still limited.

That means pricing is still a hypothesis. The first real milestone is not usage alone. It is usage plus conversion plus retention.

### Board Recommendation

The company should operate like a wedge business, not a platform business.

The near-term goal is not to maximise feature breadth. The goal is to prove that one narrow product loop creates repeatable value for one narrow buyer.

The board should care most about these metrics:

1. Weekly active therapists.
2. Repeat sessions per child.
3. Therapist review completion rate.
4. Time to review a session.
5. Therapist retention after the first month.
6. Pilot-to-paid conversion.
7. Gross margin under real usage.

If those numbers move, the company has something.

If they do not, the answer is not more features.

## Google-Style Product Strategy Critique

### Diagnosis

Wulo has built more product than most early teams, but it has not yet earned the right to behave like a multi-surface platform.

The central mistake to avoid is confusing product completeness with product certainty.

Right now the company does not need more optionality. It needs sharper focus.

### What To Cut Or De-Prioritise

#### 1. Multi-Segment Storytelling

Do not message equally to therapists, parents, schools, local authorities, and NHS buyers at the same time.

Pick therapists first. Everyone else is downstream.

#### 2. Platform-Like Complexity That Does Not Improve The Core Loop

Memory systems, recommendation explanations, institutional insight layers, and advanced planning flows may become valuable later.

But if they do not materially improve therapist activation, review frequency, retention, or willingness to pay in the next 90 days, they are not first-order priorities.

#### 3. Public-Sector Ambition As An Operating Focus

Keep the school and NHS narrative alive, but do not let it consume roadmap capacity before therapist adoption is working.

#### 4. Parent-First Framing

Parents may influence usage, but they are not the cleanest initial buyer. Parent delight matters, but parent-led positioning should not drive the strategy.

### What To Focus On First

#### 1. Nail One Magic Loop

The loop should be:

1. Therapist assigns a targeted exercise.
2. Child completes guided practice.
3. Therapist reviews the result in under two minutes.
4. Therapist uses that review to decide the next session or homework target.

If this loop is fast, credible, and sticky, Wulo has a business.

#### 2. Obsess Over Therapist Activation And Retention

The company should know exactly where therapists drop off:

- after signup
- after child creation
- after first session launch
- after first review
- before second-week usage
- before payment

That funnel matters more than adding another intelligent feature.

#### 3. Make Review Quality The Product Centre Of Gravity

The child session creates engagement.

The therapist review creates commercial value.

The review experience should feel obviously useful, fast, and trustworthy.

#### 4. Charge Earlier Than Feels Comfortable

Early payment is a product signal.

A company that delays charging often delays learning. Even discounted design-partner pricing is better than free usage with vague enthusiasm.

### What To Scale After Proof

#### Phase 1: Therapist Wedge

Win with independent SLTs and small clinics.

Success looks like repeated usage, real retention, and early paid conversion.

#### Phase 2: Clinic And School Packaging

Once the therapist loop works, package it for clinics and selected schools.

At that stage, school reporting, permissions, and admin workflows matter more.

#### Phase 3: Institutional Distribution

Only after the product has small-scale proof should Wulo invest seriously in local authority, MAT, or NHS motions.

That is when compliance packs, procurement readiness, and broader reporting become leverage instead of distraction.

### Strategic Bottom Line

Wulo should stop thinking like a broad AI platform and start behaving like a disciplined vertical workflow company.

The company does not need to win the whole speech-support market now.

It needs to become indispensable to a small number of therapists first. That is the fastest path to revenue, trust, evidence, and institutional credibility.
